L'orthocatalographe

jamais je ne me rappelle j'appelle
par son nom ce que j'interpelle
jamais je ne me contiens je me contente
de ce qui constamment me tente
jamais je ne m'écorne ni l'œil ni l'oreille
je vote contre les corbeaux pour les corneilles
jamais je ne vais à la guerre
j'orne mon cœur de ma colère
jamais je ne donne de hennin à ma femme
j'aime sa chevelure comme une flamme
jamais je ne machine ni ne m'exquise
je ne m'allèche que si me grise
jamais je ne suis petit poucet
dans la douce forêt du passé
jamais je ne jette par la fenêtre
le tas gitbont de rires ou est maêtre
jamais je ne tombe dans la gueule du loup
je ne tombe jamais dans la gueule du goût
et je ne vais dans les musées que pour enlever les muselières.

— Dotremont

The Orthocatalographer

never do I recall I call
by its name what I hail
never do I contain myself I content myself
with what constantly tempts me
never do I blunt either eye or ear
I vote against the ravens for the crows
never do I go to war
I adorn my heart with my anger
never do I give a hennin to my wife
I love her hair like a flame
never do I scheme nor grow exquisite
I am only enticed if it intoxicates me
never am I Tom Thumb
in the sweet forest of the past
never do I throw out the window
the gitbont heap of laughter where one is maêtre
never do I fall into the wolf's jaws
I never fall into the jaws of taste
and I only go to museums to remove the muzzles.

— Dotremont


Editor's note: A few translation notes, since this poem is deeply rooted in French wordplay:

The poem's engine is internal word-splitting and sound-echoes — rappelle contains appelle, contiens contains contente's root, corbeaux/corneilles share cor-, musées morphs into muselières, and loup/goût rhyme. Much of this is untranslatable without inventing equivalent English puns, which would violate the meaning. I've kept the sense and structure intact instead.

gitbont and maêtre are not standard French - they appear to be deliberate coinages or misspellings consistent with the poem's title (an invented word about orthography). They've been left as they stand in the original, since they aren't "real" words to translate.
